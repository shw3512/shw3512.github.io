\section{Exercise}

(2 pt) Write (but do NOT evaluate) an integral to find the surface area of the part of the cylinder
\begin{align*}
S:
x^{2} + z^{2}
=
4
\end{align*}
lying above (i.e. with $z \geq 0$) the square $D$ in the $x y$-plane with vertices $(0,0),(2,0),(2,2),(0,2)$.

\spaceSolution{4in}{% Begin solution.
We present two methods to computing the surface area.

Method 1: Multivariable calculus. The part of the cylinder $S$ lying above the $x y$-plane (i.e. with $z \geq 0$) can be described by solving the given equation for $z$:
\begin{align*}
z
=
\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}.
\end{align*}
Viewing $z$ as a function of $x,y$, we compute its partial derivatives to be
\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}
&=
\frac{-x}{\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}},
&
\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}
&=
0.
\end{align*}
The surface area $A(S)$ of the part of the cylinder $S$ lying above the square $D$ is
\begin{align*}
A(S)
&=
\iint_{D} \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^{2} + \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)^{2}} \, \intd A
\\
&=
\iint_{D} \sqrt{1 + \frac{x^{2}}{4 - x^{2}}} \, \intd A
\\
&=
\iint_{D} \frac{2}{\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}} \, \intd A.
\end{align*}

If desired, we can use Fubini's theorem to evaluate this double integral as an iterated integral. Using the change of variables indicated by the triangle below\fontNeedsEdit{ (draw related triangle)}, we have
\begin{align*}
\sin u
&=
\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}}{2},
&
\cos u
&=
\frac{x}{2}
&
&\Rightarrow
&
\intd x
&=
-2 \sin u \, \intd u,
\end{align*}
so
\begin{align*}
A(S)
&=
\int_{y = 0}^{y = 2} \int_{x = 0}^{x = 2} \frac{2}{\sqrt{4 - x^{2}}} \, \intd x \, \intd y
\\
&=
\int_{y = 0}^{y = 2} \intd y \int_{x = 0}^{x = 2} \frac{1}{\sin u} (-2 \sin u \, \intd u)
\\
&=
2 (-2) \int_{y = 0}^{y = 2} \left[u\right]_{x = 0}^{x = 2} \, \intd y
\\
&=
-4 \left[\arccos\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\right]_{x = 0}^{x = 2}
\\
&=
-4 \left[0 - \frac{\pi}{2}\right]
=
2 \pi.
\end{align*}

Method 2: Geometry. The surface area of the part of the cylinder $S$ lying above the square $D$ is precisely $\frac{1}{4}$ of the surface area of the total cylinder $S$ from $y = 0$ to $y = 2$. This is a cylinder of radius $2$ and height $2$, so
\begin{align*}
A(S)
=
\frac{1}{4} \left[\pi (2)^{2} 2\right]
=
2 \pi.
\end{align*}
